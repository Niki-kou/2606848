The final era we will be looking at will the classical one. From here on out we have a lot more physical remnants of burial sites, therefore we can understand more from the artifacts since they paint a more complete picture. One such compelling point is that we can understand more about the social dynamic between men and women based on how either of the gender’s graves are decorated. As Dimakis Nikolas put it "deathscapes in the Classical period were spaces of intense private and personal significance, while simultaneously being shared collective sites of experience and remembrance."<sup>16</sup> The dead and their burial sites allow us to learn more about the social expectations in Classical era Athens because it was "The family must choose (or commission) a gravestone that enhances their own reputation by, paradoxically, reflecting the dead individual's status in a way that is not reliant on the status of the particular family itself."<sup>17</sup> In addition to this, there is a clear divide between men’s and women’s burial plots, with there being an emphasis on the home for women, whereas men are seen socialising, interacting with the spirt of the Poleis more. It is clear that the gender divide was extremely prevalent at the time, and of course women were not entirely hidden from the rest of society, they were forced into different social circles to the men.
in addition to having more insight on gender, the classical worlds feeling towards dying on the battlefield changed, and soldiers were now buried in cemeteries instead of being buried in the battlefield where they did. It in fact because a custom in Athens to "celebrate an elaborate public funeral for those who died in war during the course of a year’s campaigning"<sup>18</sup> This also presented the shifting belief between the Archaic and the Classical era of Ancient Greece and their changing burial traditions and practises.
