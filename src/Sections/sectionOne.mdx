We begin by discussing the very beginnings of ancient Greece. At this time, burial grounds were much simpler than the likes that crop up during the Classical Era of ancient Greece, yet it’s still clear that care has been put into the dead of the time by the items that have been found within the burial grounds along with the corpses. At the time, inhumation is the most common form of funeral, and in fact it was "it is clear that secondary burial was a regular practice"<sup>1</sup> as bodies would often be removed and either reburied in a different location or they would be "placed in an ossuary"<sup>2</sup> so that there would be space for another to be buried in the same spot. It is also important to note that while the body was oftentimes removed and moved, they usually left the skull of the deceased behind. At the time death was seen as a more everyday matter due to short life expectancy, which would explain how casual the Neolithic Greeks seem to be about the matter. Overall, this is by far the era with the least evidence we can use to recreate its environment, as there have not been many Neolithic graves uncovered despite the fact that so many Greeks were living and dying at the time, making this our least documented area in terms of what we can learn from the burial grounds.
