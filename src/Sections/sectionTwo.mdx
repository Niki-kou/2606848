We continue on to the next major milestone for burial grounds in Ancient Greece, the Mycenaean era. This is when extravagant graves begin to take form, as evidenced by the growing popularity of the shaft graves, which in a way are an evolution to the previous pit graves. It is also important to note that despite the use of inhumation in most of ancient Greece, Athens was unique in that they preferred to cremate their dead instead since the neolithic age, as seen by the burial urn found in Attica, although this too slightly shifted during this era, with inhumation becoming more common till it became the main way to deal with the dead.

In 1876, archaeologists Heinrich Schliemann and Panagiotis Stamatakis uncovered what is now known as grave circle A, one of the most important sites in regard to the evolution of the burial settlements of Mycenaean Greece. It consisted of six shaft graves filled with lavish items such as golden masks, an "extraordinary arsenal of weapons"<sup>7</sup> with the swords being "the swords are exceptionally beautiful"<sup>8</sup> and that "and an extraordinary number of them were found" 9and jewellery with them. Clearly, this shows that there is a grander approach to death in the Mycenaean period, even if it could only be afforded by the elite of society. Furthermore, this shift to grander graves shows a return to kingship in the Mycenaean Era, as elaborate graves such as the grave circle would only have been meant for the ruling elite of the Mycenaean kingdom.
Despite this growing grandeur, a lot of Mycenaeans would not be able to afford such a lavish place, and the graves of the everyday person remained largely the same. They did though appear to have more offering in the grave with them, as in many unearthed graves we have found "vessels with food and drink"<sup>10</sup> that "may be the residue of a funeral feast, though it was evidently expected that the dead would need supplies for the afterlife."<sup>11</sup> It is also at this age that we are finally beginning to learn more about the rituals of the funerals, as more funerary literature is being written for the time. The Mycenaean era was the era of heroes after all; therefore we can learn a lot about how funerals and burials operate based on the literature written at the time.
