After the archaic era had begun, we start to see a shift away from the previously common group graves and into individual graves all over Greece. However, Athen still stood out amongst the city states, and as Christopher Mee put it, "the ‘citizen cemetery’ with modest tombs and grave goods is a feature of many Greek cities in the seventh century, such as Argos and Corinth.
However Athens rejected this new civic ideology in favor of more exclusive practices."<sup>12</sup> Once again cremation was back in style for the Athenians after a period of preferring inhumation, but this time it came with a twist; "the dead were now cremated on a pyre in the grave."<sup>13</sup> This shows a mashup between their century old tradition of cremation and traditional inhumation that took place in the rest of Greece. There are many reasons for this, but a very likely reason for the cremations is the fact that Athens simply had a much larger population than the rest of the Greek city-states, and the cremations were perhaps to save space. Along with Kerameikos, there was another cemetery named Phaleron, further showing the scale of the amount of people living in Athens during the Archaic era. 
In addition to returning to cremations, Athens also began to have more decorated grave markers, drawing more attention to the gravestones as it was an "unquestionably public space"<sup>14</sup>  so they wanted to appeal "to the wayfarer to stop, read, feel pity, to notice and remember the dead."<sup>15</sup> While the last quote does specifically refer to the classical era, there is almost no doubt that it also applied to the Archaic era burial grounds too, as that is when burial grounds began to grow in extravagance in Athens.
